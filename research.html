What?

One does not exaggerate when one says that hydrogen bonding is a crucial ingredient of life. Hydrogen bonds come in many flavors giving rise to the remarkably quirky properties of water, they stand behind the reason why the DNA double helix holds together and why proteins take on so many different shapes and have so many different functions. At the same time, hydrogen being a very light nucleus results in significant nuclear quantum effects leading to a structrual and dynamic behavior beyond the explanation offered by classical mechanics. We are interested in a theoretical description of various hydrogen bonded systems at the level of atoms including quantum nuclear effects.

How?

Our methodology is rooted in molecular dynamics simulations—a numerical treatment of equations of motion due to a system Hamiltonian resulting in a prediction of molecular behavior in the form of a trajectory through time. Specifically, we approach intermolecular interactions by ab initio methods and include the crucial quantum nuclear effects by mapping the quantum system onto a classical ring polymer abstraction through the Feynman path integral formalism. Such accuracy is traded off for extreme computational demands and is thus practically limited only to the smallest systems. To overcome the issue, we employ a novel machine learning approach and let neural networks to do the hard work for a fraction of the original cost.
